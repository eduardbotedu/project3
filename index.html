My Favorite Place: Iceland

Iceland is my favorite place. Known as the “Land of Fire and Ice,” this small island country in the North Atlantic feels like another planet. The towns are small and quiet, the people are kind, and life is closely connected to nature. Iceland offers a breathtaking mix of natural wonders, volcanoes, glaciers, waterfalls, geysers, and hot springs. Whether you're watching the northern lights or soaking in the warm Blue Lagoon, the beauty feels almost unreal. What draws me most to Iceland is how its nature felt peaceful and grounding. Being there brought me back to earth. My problems felt small compared to the vastness around me. Iceland recharged me in a way no other place ever has. The silence, the vast wilderness, and the raw beauty make it unlike anywhere else I’ve ever been.

Vík

Vík is a small coastal village located on Iceland’s southern tip. It’s known for its dramatic landscapes, surrounded by black sand beaches, towering cliffs, and volcanic rock formations. Despite its size, Vík is a popular stop for travelers due to its breathtaking views and proximity to some of Iceland’s most iconic natural landmarks.

  • Reynisdrangar: The striking coastal area near the village of Vík is famous for its black sand beaches, dramatic cliffs, powerful waves, and the iconic basalt sea stacks just offshore. The entire area is shaped by centuries of volcanic activity and ocean erosion, creating unique rock formations like columnar basalt and caves. According to local legend, the sea stacks were once trolls frozen by sunlight as they tried to drag a ship to shore. With its haunting natural beauty and mythological roots, Reynisdrangar is both a geological wonder and a deeply atmospheric place that captures the essence of Iceland’s wild south coast.
  • Viking Park: Located 11 km from Vík í Mýrdal, it sits at the southernmost tip of the country. Rather than a traditional theme park, Viking Park spans 11,540 hectares in the scenic Mýrdalshreppur area. It offers an abundance of natural and historical attractions, including the Viking grave of Hjörleifur atop Hjörleifshöfði, a Viking ship, the famous Yoda Cave, and a variety of scenic hiking trails with stunning views of Katla Glacier. Visitors are invited to immerse themselves in the rich Viking history of the region and enjoy breathtaking views from landmarks like Hjörleifshöfði and Hafursey.

The Westfjords

The Westfjords of Iceland are one of the country's most remote and untouched regions, offering dramatic landscapes and a sense of deep solitude. Characterized by steep mountains, narrow fjords, and rugged coastlines, the area is rich in natural beauty and wildlife. Visitors can witness puffins nesting along towering sea cliffs, soak in natural hot springs like Hellulaug, and explore cascading waterfalls such as Dynjandi. One of the region’s most unique treasures is Rauðisandur, a striking red-sand beach stretching for miles, offering a stark contrast to Iceland’s typically black volcanic shores. With fewer tourists than other parts of Iceland, the Westfjords provide a peaceful, off-the-beaten-path experience.

My favorite places in the Westfjords

  • Rauðisandur: meaning “Red Sand,” is a stunning and secluded beach in Iceland’s Westfjords, known for its unique golden-pink sands that shift in color with the light and tide. Stretching nearly 10 kilometers along Breiðafjörður Bay, this shoreline offers breathtaking views of the surrounding mountains. Unlike Iceland’s more common black sand beaches, Rauðisandur’s warm hues come from crushed seashells, creating a peaceful and otherworldly atmosphere. Visitors often spot seals lounging near the shore, and the area is close to the famous Látrabjarg cliffs, home to thousands of seabirds. To reach Rauðisandur, you'll have to take a scenic drive down a winding gravel road — the journey is well worth it.
  • Dynjandi: often called the "Jewel of the Westfjords," is one of Iceland’s most breathtaking waterfalls, cascading 100 meters down a mountainside. Located in the remote Arnarfjörður fjord, Dynjandi is not just a single waterfall but a series of seven falls, each with its own name, that visitors encounter during a short 15-minute hike from the parking area. The trail winds past smaller waterfalls like Bæjarfoss and Göngumannafoss before arriving at Dynjandi. As it widens from 30 meters at the top to about 60 meters at the base, the sound of the rushing water fills the valley, creating a dramatic and unforgettable spectacle.

Stokksnes

Stokksnes: is a dramatic coastal headland in southeast Iceland, known for its black sand dunes and the towering Vestrahorn mountain, often called “Batman Mountain” for its jagged, silhouette. The area offers a blend of sharp peaks, wind-carved dunes, and reflective tidal flats that create mirror-like images, especially at sunrise or during the Northern Lights. Accessible via a gravel road near Höfn, visitors can explore the beach, photograph the landscape, and even walk through a replica Viking village used as a film set.

Hallgrímskirkja

Hallgrímskirkja:is a striking Lutheran church located in Reykjavík and is one of Iceland’s most famous and recognizable landmarks. Rising 74.5 meters (244 feet) into the sky, it’s the tallest church in the country and dominates the city’s skyline. The church was designed by Guðjón Samúelsson, who drew inspiration from Iceland’s dramatic landscapes, particularly the basalt columns formed by volcanic activity. Construction began in 1945 and took over 40 years to complete. Today, Hallgrímskirkja is not only a place of worship but also a popular tourist destination, with an observation tower that offers sweeping views of Reykjavík, the mountains, and the Atlantic Ocean. Its modernist, almost futuristic appearance makes it a symbol of Icelandic identity and innovation.
